THE DERIVATIVE

From basic math to algebra 1 to calculus, everyone has probably heard of the term:

SLOPE

So let's talk about it:

Take the following scenario:
You're given 10 apples in front of you and have to eat as many as you can in a minute:





Looks like this person ate 5 apples in one minute, which seems pretty fast.

We normally regard slope as how steep a line is, as that's probably how everyone has been taught initially.
But in context, slope is a "rate of change," or in mathematical terms, final condition minus initial condition.
For example, if we do the math here and use the slope formula (y2-y1) / (x2-x1), we get -5, which in context means:
The amount of apples are being eaten at a rate of 5 per minute.





But what about a graph more complicated?


Seems pretty steep initially and gradually reduces after a while.
It's like you can't eat apples as fast anymore after stuffing a few in your mouth very quickly...
How are we going to calculate slope with this?

Originally, the x-axis of the graph above had an interval from 0-10.
We halved that, and now the interval is from 0-5.
It still has a curve, respectively. Let's do that again:



Now the interval is from 0-2.5.
So what exactly is the purpose in doing this?




We've halved the interval one more time: Now it's from 0-1.25.
And woah! All of a sudden the curvey graph we had before now looks like a straight line!

So what do we notice here?

We've taken a graph and decreased the interval that we're looking at and focusing on one specific instant.
So if we kept halving and halving these intervals, we would have a slope at an interval of 1/infinity.
This allows us to calculate slope at a very specific instant. We would call this an "instantaneous" rate of change.
Doing this allows us to calculates rates of change with very complex graphs.
